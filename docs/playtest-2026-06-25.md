# Village Playtest 2026-06-25

## Findings

- `Esc` immediately closed the client instead of cancelling the current action.
- The 25-resident milestone took roughly one minute at the default speed.
- Placing one of every production building plus a few houses was sufficient.
- Starting stock and high production rates made transport and shortages largely
  irrelevant.
- The settlement inspector was overloaded and used unclear cumulative-flow
  abbreviations (`P`, `C`, and `T`).
- Building construction costs were visible, but production inputs, outputs, and
  cycle rates were not.
- Ten-pixel map tiles made placement unnecessarily difficult.
- The starting road occupied map row zero, making it look like the northern map
  boundary.

## Implemented Response

- `Esc` now cancels placement first and clears selection second. Only the window
  close action exits.
- Default speed was reduced from 10 to 2 simulation minutes per rendered frame.
- Mouse-wheel map zoom now ranges from 8 to 32 pixels per tile and preserves the
  tile beneath the cursor.
- The starting settlement moved twenty tiles into the map and the initial camera
  frames terrain on both sides of the road.
- The inspector spells out production, consumption, transportation, logistics,
  and construction labels and hides inactive operational sections.
- Selected buildings show cycle inputs, outputs, duration, and full-staff daily
  capacity.
- Construction-menu rows show operational recipes in addition to construction
  requirements.
- Starting timber was reduced to eight units. The woodcutter is a labor-only
  bootstrap building, and subsequent construction depends on transported timber.
- One farm, woodcutter, and bakery chain supports only about sixteen residents.
  The 25-resident milestone now requires two chains.
- The automated scenario verifies that one chain fails and two chains complete
  the milestone without hunger.

## Next Playtest Questions

- Is the two-chain requirement understandable from daily bread demand and recipe
  capacity?
- Does the first shortage identify the missing farm, bakery, woodcutter, worker,
  or hauler clearly?
- Is default speed now useful, or does construction feel slow before the first
  bakery starts?
- Does zoom make placement comfortable at both the default and maximum levels?
- Is the reduced settlement summary readable without losing required detail?

## Second Playtest Findings

- The revised pacing and inspector were substantially clearer.
- Transport markers were visible for too little time and moved directly between
  building centers instead of following the road.
- A one-chain settlement stopped at 16 residents even with free housing. This is
  expected balance: one bakery produces 16 bread per day, 16 residents consume
  16 bread per day, and resident 17 requires a surplus. The inspector did not
  expose that exact threshold.

## Second Playtest Response

- The map now reconstructs a deterministic road route between connected
  buildings for renderer use.
- The transport overlay caches that route per visible job, moves cargo along the
  road, and finishes the animation even when the simulation completes and
  removes the job between rendered frames.
- Completed deliveries remain visible briefly at their destination before
  fading.
- The settlement summary now shows stored bread against the exact bread reserve
  required for the next citizen, such as `16 / 17`.
- Route contiguity, overlay retention, and the population-growth bread
  requirement have automated coverage.

## Third Playtest Questions

- Can you follow bread, timber, grain, and firewood from source to destination at
  the default speed?
- Is the transport overlay readable when several jobs share one road?
- At 16 residents, does `GROWTH BREAD: 16 / 17` make the need for a second bakery
  chain clear?
